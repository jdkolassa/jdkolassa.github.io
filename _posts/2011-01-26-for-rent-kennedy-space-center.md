[For rent: Kennedy Space Center facilities, launch pads &#8211; Science Fair: Science and Space News &#8211; USATODAY.com](http://content.usatoday.com/communities/sciencefair/post/2011/01/for-rent-kennedy-space-center-facilities-launch-pads/1).

For once, let&#8217;s not look a gift horse in the mouth.

Straight off the bat, this could be a _huge_ moneymaking opportunity for NASA. Start-up costs are a major consideration in any endeavor, and building the proper launch facilities is quite expensive, I&#8217;d imagine. But renting out the KSC from NASA, well, I dunno how much NASA would charge, but it would have to be cheaper than building your launch facilities from scratch, _and_ they&#8217;re probably better than most commercial launch sites, at least if you&#8217;re intending to send up manned spacecraft (that aren&#8217;t Burt Rutan&#8217;s _SpaceShipOne_.)

But secondly, this could also help re-ignite the drive to space. Since the 70&#8217;s we&#8217;ve been languishing in the space exploration department thanks to politics, which have bogged down any sort of continuation into the final frontier, while not really opening up the field to private organizations that are actually interested in it. See, this is the major problem with putting anything under the thumb of government: the politicians are only going to follow the majority of the public, that 51% (or less, in some cases) of voters that will put them in office, which means that if the majority of the public loses interest in space, we&#8217;re not going to do anything with it; and the minority who _are_ interested can&#8217;t do anything about it because they would have to go through an indifferent, disinterested government. At least let them use their own resources&#8211;waste them, even&#8211;on getting into space, but let them do it. What harm could come of it? In fact, their purchasing of materials and labor would probably have given the economy a boost.

But, even if we&#8217;re a few decades late on this front, better late than never. I&#8217;m quite sure all the &#8220;private&#8221; spaceflight for the next 20 years will be some sort of public-private partnership, which I am told is the &#8220;worst of both worlds,&#8221; but I feel it must be better than having a fully public program. At least we&#8217;ll be getting people up there, and onto more than just the ISS. Who knows, maybe&#8230;the Moon? Mars? Or even the stars? I&#8217;m going to get lumped with other sci-fi writers out there for saying this, but so be it: it&#8217;s _vital_ we get back into space. The last space race was a huge technological leap forward for our entire planet, and with what we have now, think of what could happen if we go to another one. Teflon is so 1980s; tomorrow we&#8217;ll have transparisteel! But not only that, there&#8217;s also the environment to consider. I&#8217;m not exactly the biggest greenie weenie out there, but I still care about Mother Earth, and its clear that we&#8217;re poisoning her, no doubt about it. We&#8217;re also running low on natural resources, at least those we need to sustain our global society, so we need to start looking for other places to get resources from.

And finally there&#8217;s the whole issue of just having an offworld colony as a &#8220;backup.&#8221; I don&#8217;t really think we&#8217;re going to have a mass extinction event in the next 100 years, but even so, there&#8217;s no harm in having an offworld colony. It might actually be good, stimulating trade, and most importantly, a sense of adventure that I feel has not been nourished very well for the past generation; sure, we feel it, but the only time it gets satiated is in a movie theater or playing a video game. No, I&#8217;m talking about _really_ taking care of it. Then there&#8217;s the political possibilities; the last time we had a major colonization effort in the &#8220;New World,&#8221; there was a revolution and the establishment of the first modern representative democracy, with limited government and checks and balances. If we start colonizing Mars&#8211;or just space in general if we want to go the orbital habitat route, although that&#8217;s more effort-intensive&#8211;who knows what sort of political and societal revolutions we&#8217;ll engender. It could be the spark to re-ignite a stagnate global civilization. But we won&#8217;t know if we don&#8217;t go out there.

I look upon this with great interest and great hope. I&#8217;m sure something will get botched up, it always does, but hopefully there will be more good than bad coming out of this.
