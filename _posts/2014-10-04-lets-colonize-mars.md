So Elon Musk wants to screw Earth and colonize Mars. Excellent, I completely agree. Let&#8217;s get started.

The <a href="http://aeon.co/magazine/technology/the-elon-musk-interview-on-mars/" target="_blank">interview Musk gave to Ross Anderson of Aeon Magazine is fantastic</a>. It&#8217;s been a long time since I&#8217;ve read such a forceful advocacy for space colonization, which is refreshing. It seems like the cause of space has languished over the past couple of decades while people want to focus on more down to Earth matters. I think they&#8217;re forgetting that many of our down to Earth matters could probably be solved by going outward and exploring new frontiers &#8211; and settling them!

My reasons are different than Musk&#8217;s, are, though. Musk seems to be afraid that, since we haven&#8217;t discovered any interstellar aliens in our searches of the night sky, something bad must have happened to all of them:

> <span style="color: #000000;">Musk has a more sinister theory [<em>to the Fermi Paradox, basically &#8211;Jeremy</em>]. ‘The absence of any noticeable life may be an argument in favour of us being in a simulation,’ he told me. ‘Like when you’re playing an adventure game, and you can see the stars in the background, but you can’t ever get there. If it’s not a simulation, then maybe we’re in a lab and there’s some advanced alien civilisation that’s just watching how we develop, out of curiosity, like mould in a petri dish.’ Musk flipped through a few more possibilities, each packing a deeper existential chill than the last, until finally he came around to the import of it all. ‘If you look at our current technology level, something strange has to happen to civilisations, and I mean strange in a bad way,’ he said. ‘And it could be that there are a whole lot of dead, one-planet civilisations.’</span>

Personally, I&#8217;m more in favor of the Great Filter being life itself. _Wait But Why_ [has a great blog post on the Fermi Paradox and all of its implications](http://waitbutwhy.com/2014/05/fermi-paradox.html), and count me as a guy who thinks that life is much harder to happen than Ross Anderson seems to think (going off what he writes in Aeon; it might be he&#8217;s just summarizing what others think and that&#8217;s not his own opinion.) I don&#8217;t look at this as a bad thing; instead, we now have the entire cosmos open to ourselves. _We_ are the Ancients, the Precursors, the Progenitors of life in a barren and empty universe.

But not if we screw it up before we get out there.

I&#8217;m not talking about the existential fears that most people talk about. I&#8217;m not worried about nuclear war or plague or global warming killing us. To be sure, we have some problems for this century: we need to stamp out religious and ideological extremism that leads to violence; find new and renewable sources of energy to keep powering our civilization; and maybe not build artificial superintelligences in our basements. But I think these (well, to one extent or another) are all manageable. The problem I fear is one of philosophy, political science, and sociology. We need space colonization to overcome the dimming of the (classical) liberal vision.

I&#8217;ve been thinking about this topic for a long, long time. Well, over a year, to be more exact, but it&#8217;s been fluttering in my head for longer. The problem is that I&#8217;m finding it very hard to put it into words _why_ we must colonize Mars &#8211; and the rest of space &#8211; to preserve classical liberalism and by extension civilization, freedom, and all those good things.

I look at the growth of government over the past century and I see it as expansion turning inwards. There is less for us to go out and explore, now. We no longer have a frontier, a Wild West where the government&#8217;s arm is distant and individuals rely on themselves. It seems very romantic, because it is very romantic &#8211; and of course, there were problems. Colonization uprooted and destroyed indigenous cultures all over the world, caused pain and suffering by bringing diseases, bloodshed, and slavery. The Wild West was not as dangerous as the Western movie genre made it out to be, but there was racism, crime, and an eye for an eye mentality in some parts. My point, being, though, was that as there was a frontier, there was an argument for freedom. Government could not expand inwards on people because there was somewhere to expand outwards.

But then the 20th century came. By now, there was nowhere left to expand to. The only uncolonized parts of our world are the Artic, the Antartic, and the bottom of the oceans &#8211; the first two being extremely inhospitable and undesirable, the latter uninhabitable until somebody decides to invent _SeaQuest_ in the real world. (Get on that, Musk.) Now, the expanding mass of government ran up against a solid wall, and as it hit this wall it folded back in on itself and expanded back towards its center. Now it was expanding on top of itself, layering itself upon itself, burying beneath itself the seeds of liberalism and freedom. Where else could it go now but onto its own people?

We lost the frontier. On top of that, we continued to multiply. I hate thinking in this manner, but the law of supply and demand comes back to haunt me. We have all these people now, and we keeping having more, and I wonder, as supply goes up, does demand go _down_? It used to be you could know everyone in your community. Now, do we just look at others as statistics? Not even fully autonomous human beings? Do we think everyone around us is a p-zombie? It seems very crass on one hand &#8211; how can we apply supply and demand to _people_ &#8211; and yet very conservative on the other &#8211; here I am talking about community and how the modern era has increased the distance between us and yadda yadda yadda. Not being that sort of conservative &#8211; or really, any conservative at all &#8211; it&#8217;s hard for me to put this into words.

Then there was the toddler <a href="http://www.abcactionnews.com/news/world/bystander-effect-may-explain-why-chinese-toddler-was-left-injured-on-the-road" target="_blank">who was run over by two vehicles and ignored by scores of passersby</a> before finally receiving help. Again, this is from China.

These are just the two things that come to the top of my mind. I don&#8217;t know if it&#8217;s because there are a lot of people in China, if there&#8217;s something deeper in Chinese culture, or if these are really bad examples. But that is what I think of when I see rising population. Is this something we can overcome? Is it bound to happen?

Then there is the issue of running out of work for people. I know many scoff at the idea, but there is some concern of &#8220;technological unemployment&#8221;. My friend Travis Thornton has <a href="http://travisthornton.net/2013/05/01/fear-and-eudaimonia-in-the-singularity/" target="_blank">blogged about</a> this <a href="http://travisthornton.net/2013/11/14/overcoming-the-post-scarcity-blues/" target="_blank">subject before</a>. Now personally I am all in favor of a post-scarcity economy, and I think it&#8217;s absolutely delightful that we&#8217;re heading towards one&#8230;but are we going to need a new thing to give us meaning? I realize these thoughts are not entirely coherent or cogent. Like I said, I&#8217;m having difficulty putting what I&#8217;m thinking and feeling into words. That&#8217;s why I&#8217;m doing this blog post, to solicit feedback and comments and see if I&#8217;m on the right track. But essentially, what I see is that, to preserve classical liberalism, individual freedom, and a culture of the same, we need to start colonizing planets. We need to go with Musk and start doing this _right now_. It doesn&#8217;t necessarily have to be Mars. We should also colonize the Moon (<a href="http://www.slate.com/articles/technology/future_tense/2014/07/terraforming_the_moon_it_would_be_a_lot_like_florida.html" target="_blank">though terraforming</a> it would be a waste of time I think, since it doesn&#8217;t have enough gravity to hold on to an atmosphere, unless you <a href="http://www.orionsarm.com/eg-article/47bcf1bdc2cef" target="_blank">paraterraform</a>), and we should probably also build O&#8217;Neill and McKendree Cylinders. Eventually, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terraforming_of_Venus" target="_blank">we might even terraform Venus</a>, build <a href="http://www.orionsarm.com/eg-article/4845ef5c4ca7c" target="_blank">Banks Orbitals</a> and a Ringworld (okay, fine, we can have one Halo off in the corner for all the first person shooter types) and then from there&#8230;

The galaxy will be our oyster.

But not if we get stuck here. It&#8217;s not the asteroids that will kill us, or the threat of alien invasion, or potential nuclear war or grey goo or artificial superintelligence. If anything does us in, it will be the banal overlayering of bureaucratic, authoritarian government, run by busybodies and people of little vision. Humanity needs a new frontier, and there are many out there: uninhabited, barren, lifeless, ready for us to come. We need that frontier to rekindle our spirit of freedom, and get us moving again. Take the germ of liberalism, and spread it across the stars.

That&#8217;s my vision for the future. And that means I&#8217;m right there with Elon Musk. Let&#8217;s go to Mars.
