While checking the online thesaurus for another blog post on United Liberty, I saw the following blog post there about children and swearing. I&#8217;ve had my thoughts about it for a long time, particularly because of the censorship that went on whenever Japanese cartoons were brought over to America. (And these were cartoons that were primarily aimed at preteens in Japan, by the way.) [The blog says thusly](http://hotword.dictionary.com/swearingtoddle/?__utma=1.2020920858.1322979241.1325792398.1327255531.3&__utmb=1.1.10.1327255531&__utmc=1&__utmx=-&__utmz=1.1322979241.1.1.utmcsr=google|utmccn=(organic)|utmcmd=organic|utmctr=retirement%20home%20%2B%20thesaurus&__utmv=-&__utmk=119900424):

> Causing a bigger ruckus than Countdown, the popular American sitcom, Modern Family featured a swearing toddler in their latest episode. In this case, it was not a “fleeting expletive.” The show intentionally included a cursing two-year and bleeped out all swear words. When the show premiered last year, [the _New York Times_](http://www.nytimes.com/2011/01/23/fashion/23THISLIFE.html?pagewanted=all) wrote about how accurately it reflects contemporary American families, and this storyline is an accurate portrait of what many parents go through. With the profanity [conundrum](http://dictionary.reference.com/browse/conundrum?__utma=1.2020920858.1322979241.1327255531.1327255540.4&__utmb=1.1.10.1327255540&__utmc=1&__utmx=-&__utmz=1.1322979241.1.1.utmcsr=google|utmccn=(organic)|utmcmd=organic|utmctr=retirement%20home%20%2B%20thesaurus|utmcct=/&__utmv=-&__utmk=90088475), parents of young children may feel an uncanny sense of familiarity. Just as your three-year-old is learning to form complete sentences, to say words like “almost,” and generally to absorb all language around her, she will accidentally [acquire](http://dictionary.reference.com/browse/acquire?__utma=1.2020920858.1322979241.1327255531.1327255540.4&__utmb=1.1.10.1327255540&__utmc=1&__utmx=-&__utmz=1.1322979241.1.1.utmcsr=google|utmccn=(organic)|utmcmd=organic|utmctr=retirement%20home%20%2B%20thesaurus|utmcct=/&__utmv=-&__utmk=90088475) some dirty words.
> 
> But, as a parent of a swearing child, what would you do? Even if the parent does not swear in front of the child, there are many other opportunities – around older siblings, or even aunts and uncles, and out in public – for children to learn foul language. On Modern Family, the toddler’s dad cannot stop laughing at the situation. In real life, it is hard to explain to a child what “bad” words are. The child will inevitably ask, Why? And that’s a really hard question. Why are some words “bad” and other words “good”? In some cases we have euphemisms to code otherwise [illicit](http://dictionary.reference.com/browse/illicit?__utma=1.2020920858.1322979241.1327255531.1327255540.4&__utmb=1.1.10.1327255540&__utmc=1&__utmx=-&__utmz=1.1322979241.1.1.utmcsr=google|utmccn=(organic)|utmcmd=organic|utmctr=retirement%20home%20%2B%20thesaurus|utmcct=/&__utmv=-&__utmk=90088475) topics. (Learn more about euphemisms [here](http://hotword.dictionary.com/occupy/).)
> 
> Jimmy Fallon’s segment “Shootin’ the Bleep” mocks the use of bleeps to disguise profanity. He highlights the fact that when a word is bleeped on television, the vast majority of the audience knows what word is being censored. So what’s the point? Obviously the point is to preserve decorum despite what is actually there.

That is quite true: what is the point? What is the point of swearing, or stopping people from swearing?

I&#8217;ll be honest and say there is a bit too much &#8220;swearing&#8221; going on in America. Many times, people drop the f-bomb for absolutely no reason, or the s-word for similarly stupid non-reasons. (&#8220;Hey man, put the shit in your bag and let&#8217;s go!&#8221;) For me, swearing is used as an extreme emphasis, to communicate utter rage or mind-boggling confusion. It is useful, though like a strong spice, only when used sparingly. When used more often, it tends to use its strength and becomes yet another milquetoast part of our vocabulary.

But on the flip side, I think trying to shield children from all forms of vulgarities and obscenities is counterproductive. Eventually, they&#8217;re going to encounter these things. That&#8217;s life. Trying to put off when they will finally deal with it doesn&#8217;t help them. They should slowly weaned onto these concepts, taught about them (in ways beyond &#8220;Watch your mouth!&#8221;), and exposed. They will encounter these words anyways, whether from their friends, other adults, or the media, and believe me, by the time they take their first year into adolescence, they will probably know all of them quite well. So trying to &#8220;protect&#8221; them is just a waste of time.

And the bleeps? What good do the bleeps do? Half the time you know _exactly_ what word was used, and the other half you&#8217;re guessing with a pretty good idea. The bleeps do _nothing_ but add comedy, if at that. The obscenity rules in place do nothing more than make movies and TV just slightly more unrealistic (TV more so.)

We should stop it with this nonsense. We should stop babying our children&#8211;obviously, let&#8217;s not teach toddlers the f-bomb, but let&#8217;s not try to clamp down on teenagers so damn much either. Swearing is disgusting, but not any more than any hundreds of other evils. If you want a reason for moral decay in this society, it&#8217;s not vulgarities on the television&#8211;it&#8217;s rather from infantilizing people and removing their sense of responsibility. Just treat people like adults and get over it.
