A random bit of writing I did on a piece of paper one day. It would be the opening for a novel form of that short story I worked on last year, called &#8220;Janzer.&#8221; It might happen. It might not.

<!--more-->

By all accounts, Major Cerran was less a man that he was an automaton. Point him at something to destroy, and he would&#8211;no complaints, no regrets, and no failures.

He was aware what the other officers on the base thought of him. Ruthless. Cold. Emotionless. To him, they were irrelevant datapoints not worthy of storage. It wasn&#8217;t just that he did not care, he didn&#8217;t know _how_ to care. That was never part of his training.

As he walked past a gaggle of enlisted in the skywalk, the window provided a stark reminder of all he had to focus on: the rolling hills of Mechanza, long ago burned to the bedrock, with only a few rotting towers of the old city standing, their metal rib cages a warning to all who took the threats of facing this world lightly.

Cerran never spared it a glance. He had seen the same sight now for over a decade. It had no effect on him. It never did.

He took the turbolift, alone, up to level 31. He stepped out into the foyer and presented his ID to the clerk on duty. She tried her utmost to avoid interacting with him, and for his part Cerran did nothing to stop her. She just pointed to the door, Cerran nodded. He pushed it open to be greeted to a debate already in progress.

&#8220;-the resources for a fishing expedition!&#8221;

&#8220;It&#8217;s recon, Schulkze, we can afford a team. Particularly for something with a payoff as large as this.&#8221;

&#8220;Payoff? What payoff? All I see is&#8211;&#8221;

&#8220;Generals, sirs, please.&#8221; Into the fracas stepped a slender, brown-haired man, his voice clearer than a klaxon. Commander Cielan, eight years Cerran&#8217;s senior, and one who he would&#8211;if he understand it&#8211;given a hefty does of respect.

The generals, all save two, being ancient men with faces like craggy cliffs, reluctantly returned to their seats. The first exception was an ancient woman, the cybernetic implants on her back, neck, and chest keeping her permanently rooted to her chair. The second was General Arvan, and he would not sit. He was also not that old, and not craggy in the slightest. Indeed, his face was unblemished, and the deep rich brown in his eyes had not clouded a bit. Cerran noted the intensity of his stare, and concluded that Arvan would be running this meeting.

&#8220;So,&#8221; the general began, &#8220;This is the killer. Leashed.&#8221; He stepped into Cerran&#8217;s face; the major maintained his thousand yard stare. &#8220;The so-called man who has no compunctions about leaving his troops behind. No loyalty to his comrades, only a mechanistic focus on his mission.&#8221;

Cerran couldn&#8217;t pick up an invitation for comment, so he didn&#8217;t. He could just see in his peripheral vision Commander Cielan looking at him expectantly, but he wasn&#8217;t sure why.

Arvan kept staring at Cerran for another minute, then finally turned away and walked over to a spot in the first row of the briefing amphitheater.
