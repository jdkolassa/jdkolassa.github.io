I consider myself to be an intellectual. I&#8217;m more of a thinker than a lover, a fighter, or even a doer. If I had a job where I would be paid to just sit around and think all day&#8211;you know, in the bowels of some Hollywood production company&#8211;I would do just that.

Unfortunately, thinking does not get anything done in this world. Sure, it&#8217;s great to have a plan, but unless one connects that plan with actual _action_, all you get at the end of the day is a slightly thinner wallet and an unsatisfied feeling. Having had this happen to me a lot, listen to me on this: it is _deeply_ unsettling.

Yet one must think when writing a story, must they not? They must contemplate the plot, the characters, the point of view, the theme (although on that particular I digress)&#8230;if not, wouldn&#8217;t one&#8217;s work turn out a muddled mess? We&#8217;re taught from a young age, in public school, to have an outline, to focus on our key message in our writing, to have a beginning, middle, and end, and to focus on this when in the preparatory stages, which could take however long to fulfill these needs. I myself see a great benefit here from my own experience as a writer. When I was in my sophomore year of high school, I wrote the first draft of my science fiction novel&#8211;the one which I wanted to be my _magnum opus_&#8211;in about six to eight months. It was grand, I would simply sit down and out stuff would come. I had a 400 page manuscript (double-spaced), which for me of only 15 years was a crowning achievement. I&#8217;m still impressed I managed to bash that out in between school, homework, club activities, family obligations, and dealing with my own adolescence.

The problem, though, was that it was unpublishable. Looking back on it even a few weeks after it was finished, I cringed. It was god-awful. There were continuity errors all over the place, where I forgot what happened and didn&#8217;t pay attention. The dialogue was unrealistic, cheesy, and wince-inducing. Trying to put emotion into my characters was also an exercise in excess; the word &#8220;melodramatic&#8221; has never been so appropriate. Then there was the title; that was just a bad idea by definition. I looked back on this muddled mess and thought to myself, &#8220;This is because I didn&#8217;t have a plan. I didn&#8217;t think it through before I began. I just followed the whims of the moment.&#8221; And so I went back to it, writing it over, this time trying to create some sort of plot before I moved in.

Six years later, I have nothing to show for it but stress and mental anguish. Endlessly I rewrote the first half of the book, but always got stressed, succumbed to the dark side of my internal editor, and did not go anywhere. It was never good enough; it always had to be redone. I thought and thought and thought until finally I stopped banging keys on the keyboard and just walked away.

Contrast this with my performance last night. After procrastinating for several hours about this new short story I&#8217;m working on, I put away my laptop, took out my notebook&#8211;a _real_ notebook, mind you, [one of these babies](http://www.amazon.com/Mead-Notebook-1-Subject-70-Count-College/dp/B003NSB0DM/ref=sr_1_6?s=office-products&ie=UTF8&qid=1297728635&sr=1-6)&#8211;and bashed out ten pages. It was exhilarating, for someone who had been stuck in the doldrums for so long. It was like therapy, watching the words seep from my 0.9mm mechanical pencil onto the paper, the story coming to life with me only being an observer.

I simply didn&#8217;t think.

It&#8217;s a startling revelation. For me, a thinking person, the realization that said thinking is in fact my greatest  obstacle is, well, mind-blowing. And yet its also quite obvious; I think subconsciously I knew this, with said subconscious saying, the whole time, &#8220;_Dude, let me outta here! I got stuff to do!_&#8221;

The lesson to take away from all this is that one must not think when writing. Sure, during the rewriting process, it is necessary to take a critical look at one&#8217;s work and cut out that which does not fit, polish what does, and make it more consistent and coherent, but do not think. _Feel_. Stories are not really drawn from one&#8217;s brain. Opinion pieces, history books, and most nonfiction does come from one&#8217;s brain. There&#8217;s also room for literary and experimental fiction, when one just wants to see how X works or doesn&#8217;t work, and that&#8217;s fine. But for most fiction, it comes from the heart. What you feel inside is what gives fuel to your stories. For without heart, they do not live, and instead exist purely on life support. Don&#8217;t think. Write.

I hated that advice, because it just seemed like a copout but&#8230;it&#8217;s the truth. You just need to write. The best part about it is, no matter how crappy the stuff comes out is, you&#8217;ll feel _good_. If you don&#8217;t, you need to stop writing and go get another job, like jackhammering. Do whatever it is that makes you feel good&#8211;just so long as, you know, don&#8217;t break the law.

Now, I&#8217;m going to go and try to bash out at least four more pages, even though I _should_ be on life support, fighting this mammoth cold. We&#8217;ll see which one of us will win tonight.
