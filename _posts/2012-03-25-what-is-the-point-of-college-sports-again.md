While watching NCAA March Madness (for once in my life), my roommate explained to me that, when there&#8217;s a commercial during the game, they stop play. So nothing happens when the network isn&#8217;t covering it. Indeed, according to my roommate, the networks have negotiated predetermined blackout times with the NCAA so they can afford to put in short bursts of commercials without missing a moment from the game.

<div>
  <p>
    I would take no issue with this were it the NBA or the NFL. Ultimately, both organizations are purely for-profit entities whose goal is to provide entertainment and make boatloads of money off it. If there weren&#8217;t any networks, they wouldn&#8217;t really have a <em>raison d&#8217;etre</em>. (I&#8217;m sure they would, in some sense, since there were sports leagues before major cable networks, but it would be vastly different than it would be today.) But can the same thing be said about the NCAA?
  </p>
  
  <p>
    The NCAA&#8211;the National Collegiate Athletic Association&#8211;is ostensibly an organization that unites and coordinates sports at college campuses across the country. It still pays attention to the &#8220;Corinthian Ideal,&#8221; that athletes should not be paid. (Indeed, all NCAA athletes, no matter how skilled they are, are technically &#8220;amateurs,&#8221; since they are not paid, as &#8220;professionals&#8221; are.) The point of the NCAA is not really to make money. It is to provide a welcoming environment for students who decide to play sports and to provide a tournament at the end of the year where they can show off their skills and have a sense of accomplishment, even if they bombed out in the first (well, now, really the second) round.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    Another thing that really bothers me is that these players are having millions of dollars being made off their efforts, yet they really don&#8217;t see any of it. After all, they are not supposed to be paid in any way (aside from athletic scholarships, which is indirect) and can&#8217;t make any material fortunes off the merchandising based around them. This, to me, seems wrong. They deserve to reap the bounties of what they have sown, and I don&#8217;t think that getting a scholarship to college is really an adequate bounty for that.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    We have to ask ourselves, &#8220;What is the point of college?&#8221; To me, the point of college is education. You go to college to improve yourself, to learn new skills, to challenge yourself with new ideas and new horizons, and to then use this new found knowledge to go on to all new heights. Far too many, though, view college as four (or five) years (personally, I did in three) of either goofing off and just having lots of sex, booze, and drugs; or they view it as a training ground for a professional sports league. While both bother me, the latter bothers me marginally more. Are colleges accepting students because they have the capacity to learn and improve themselves, and thus bring value to society? Or are they being accepted because they can shoot some hoops or score some touchdowns?
  </p>
  
  <p>
    Don&#8217;t get me wrong, sports can bring value (<em>considerable</em> value) to society, but I don&#8217;t think  they should be doing that in college. If the NBA and the NFL really want to have development leagues where they can get new players, they should invest the time, money, and energy to make them. MLB has its farm system; futbol (the real one, which many Americans idiotically call &#8220;soccer,&#8221;) usually operates on a delegation and relegation format that works surprisingly well at ensuring new talent and teams. While there is no really no system in place for the NBA, after the Continental Basketball Association died back in &#8217;09, the NFL does have the up and coming United Football League to work with (which, unfortunately, now just has a bunch of ex-NFL players) and they could easily start a spring league for people even lower on the ladder.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    We need to dismantle the current system for college athletics, abolish athletic scholarships entirely, and move to a system with developmental leagues. It&#8217;s fine for students to play sports, but to trot them out there and just use them as pawns for schools to make money&#8211;which, actually, most of them simply don&#8217;t&#8211;is just disgusting. It makes me think of the <em>Hunger Games</em> (though, in all honesty, I haven&#8217;t read them, though I shall, eventually.) It also takes away from students who well and truly want to <em>learn</em> something, and actually have their schools&#8217; budgets go to academic instruction, rather than prettying up the campus or trying to send teams to conference championships across the country.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    This should also not be considered a knock against professional sports leagues and students who participate in the NCAA. While, when I watch the NCAA advert that says &#8220;Still think we&#8217;re dumb jocks?&#8221; and I say &#8220;Yes,&#8221; I really meant they&#8217;re just in the wrong place and should be out focusing on their real talents, instead of being in college, wasting their time. And the pro leagues definitely have their place. But the current system, as it is, cannot and should not stand. Either start playing your college players real dough for what they&#8217;re doing, or end it.
  </p>
</div>
