On 6 August 1945 – 67 years ago today – a control operator at the Japanese Broadcasting Corporation noticed that there was no signal from Radio Hiroshima. It had, seemingly, gone off air. Telephone calls couldnt reach the city centre either. There was a simple reason for this – the city centre wasnt there any more.

via [Phil Strongman: Hiroshima is a war crime that haunts my family, 67 years on &#8211; Commentators &#8211; Opinion &#8211; The Independent](http://www.independent.co.uk/opinion/commentators/phil-strongman-hiroshima-is-a-war-crime-that-haunts-my-family-67-years-on-8008821.html).

What follows after that link is a very interesting op-ed in the British _Independent_ that asserts that the United States only kept the war going so that they could test the nuclear bomb on a &#8220;virgin city.&#8221; Strongman says that the US demand of unconditional surrender was unreasonable, mainly because they later acquiesed to a &#8220;conditional&#8221; surrender later on that wouldn&#8217;t put Emperor Hirohito on trial. I think that&#8217;s a stupid thing to say, because of course any country that witnessed the horrors of what Japan did in the Pacific would demand such action. And you know&#8230;maybe Hirohito should have gone on trial. Well, really, it should have been the fascist military leaders like Tojo, but maybe we should have set Hirohito down in the chair and stripped him of his false divinity. Might have saved Japan a lot of grief in the long run.

Strongman also challenges the argument that without the nukes, Japan wouldn&#8217;t have surrendered and that hundreds of thousands of US doughboys would have lost their lives. I think his challenge is also somewhat stupid. Although he cites as evidence a contemporary news report that says &#8220;exists to prove that Japan was seeking to surrender many weeks before the atomic bomb…&#8221; we all know the veracity of news. He does cite Eisenhower, Nimitz, and Leahy, though, which is far more credible in my eyes (in particular Eisenhower, who needs to be much higher up on the list of greatest US presidents that he ever is.)

Still, though, Japanese women were strapping their children to their stomachs and jumping off of cliffs to avoid being raped by Americans (though, for all I know, some of those American doughboys were rapists.) The Japanese codified _kamikaze_ suicide attacks. They were literally throwing away their lives at every second for this war. If we hadn&#8217;t laid down such a smackdown that blew away their minds so utterly, I doubt there would have been any Japan left. Let&#8217;s face it: at that time period, the whole of Japan was _insane_.

That&#8217;s not to say the nuclear weapons were a perfect policy. I&#8217;ve always had a weird view: the one bomb on Hiroshima, while regrettable, was probably necessary to get Japan to wake up and realize what it was in. Nagasaki, on the other hand, was truly unnecessary and really is a shame. I mean, three days between attacks of such a magnitude that nobody can even comprehend it? What gives? You would have to give the Japanese more time to respond, and that would take extra time since you blew out all their telephones and they just don&#8217;t understand what happened. So Hiroshima yes, Nagasaki no.

My only question is why can&#8217;t people understand this? It&#8217;s really quite simple.
