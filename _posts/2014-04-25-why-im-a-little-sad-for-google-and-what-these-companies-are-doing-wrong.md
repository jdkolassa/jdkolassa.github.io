[Report: Google to end forced G+ integration, drastically cut division resources | Ars Technica](http://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2014/04/report-google-to-end-forced-g-integration-drastically-cut-division-resources/)

Google+ seems to be the red-headed stepchild of social media networks &#8211; an image not helped by the fact that its branding is all, well, red. But in any case, Google+ has never received the support and attention that other networks &#8211; hell, even LinkedIn &#8211; have received over the years. It&#8217;s been &#8220;that Google thing&#8221; and while you would think because it has freaking **<span style="text-decoration: underline;">GOOGLE</span>** in the name _everyone_ would be using it, they haven&#8217;t. Now, Google is appearing to &#8220;pivot&#8221; away from Google+, first with G+&#8217;s leader leaving the company, and then this news that possibly over 1,000 Google employees will be shifted around. According to the linked story, TechCrunch is calling Google+ the &#8220;walking dead.&#8221;

Yikes.

There are a lot of great things about Google+ that I like. <a href="http://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2014/04/report-google-to-end-forced-g-integration-drastically-cut-division-resources/?comments=1&post=26715465#comment-26715465" target="_blank">This commenter on the post says a lot of them</a>. One of the things s/he says I will also say: I do not live on Google+. But for an occasional trip, I can read a lot of interesting people&#8217;s interesting thoughts. People like Google&#8217;s own Larry Page and Sergey Brin, the inventor of Linux Linux Torvalds, a few podcasters I follow, and some very fascinating groups on roleplaying games, Linux, computer programming, and science fiction. Google+ also has no character limit, so you can write screeds if you wish, and it does seem like a breath of fresh air to get away from the increasingly stale and stultifying Facebook atmosphere and the hectic, sweltering rapid-fire heat of Twitter.

Perhaps the greatest part of Google+ from a publisher standpoint, however, is how well it integrates with Google search. +1 a story you like, and you push that story higher in the search rankings whenever someone searches for the story&#8217;s topic. +1 really helps get stories to the top, as they&#8217;re usually recommended by your friends (or, at least, in theory they are.) Of course, you can +1 your own stories and help they grow as well. It&#8217;s a fantastic idea.

Unfortunately, Google may some serious errors. One of them was the forced integration with the YouTube comments. Look, hateful, dumb, anonymous YouTube comments are an Internet _institution_. They are worse than even 4chan, and that&#8217;s almost an Xbox achievement. And yet people want them to be that way, and they don&#8217;t want their real names and faces attached. Destroying that pissed off a _massive_ Internet community, one that&#8217;s arguably larger and more entrenched than even Facebook. Not a smart move, Google. Forced integration with other services beyond search was also starting to grind on a lot of people&#8217;s nerves.

My personal pet peeve, however, is how Google has been so damn stingy with the personal profile API. Although they allowed some services, such as Buffer (which I highly recommend, by the way) to post to Google+ pages, but so far it hasn&#8217;t let anyone else post to Google+ personal profiles. There was, for some time, an Android app called &#8220;Jift&#8221; that allowed you to simultaneously post to Twitter, Facebook, and Google+, but it was pulled after awhile (and it&#8217;s style of reading posts from all three services wasn&#8217;t that great to begin with.) This forces people to get out of their main app, which usually can post to Twitter and Facebook simultaneously (like, say, <a href="https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.levelup.touiteur" target="_blank">Plume</a>), and go into the Google+ app. You know who wants to take that extra step? _Nobody_. Why not just do it all in one go? That would make it dramatically easier to use Google+, although it would cut down on the number of people actually using the Google+ app.

Which is why Google hates it.

This brings me to what I think all of these companies are doing wrong: they are not providing _choices_. That&#8217;s what a consumer wants, right? A consumer, in most instances, wants a _choice_ to make. And I don&#8217;t see why companies can&#8217;t do this. You have Google+ forcing itself on YouTube commentators and in other places; you have Facebook now forcing people to get a separate app on their phone to use Facebook messages and soon a separate app for events and possibly even groups as well. You have them changing up user interfaces all the time with no option to go to an older one that some users prefer. It&#8217;s this forcing of things that is really starting to annoy people, though I wonder if it will truly bite these companies in the behind, because while annoyed, most people still go along with the changes. Still, how can providing your users more choices ever be a bad thing?

There&#8217;s the old saw that &#8220;The user isn&#8217;t the customer, the user is the product&#8221; but I don&#8217;t know how much I buy that. Facebook runs on its users and it can&#8217;t get anywhere without them, so it can&#8217;t annoy them _too _much (although the line of no return seems to keep receding into the distance day by day.) Google as well, though I&#8217;ll be honest I have no freaking clue how Twitter makes money. (They can&#8217;t be selling that many promoted accounts and tweets, can they?)

I hope Google+ sticks around. (I really hopes it overcomes Facebook some day, so I can actually escape.) I hope it continues. But we&#8217;ll have to see, won&#8217;t we?
