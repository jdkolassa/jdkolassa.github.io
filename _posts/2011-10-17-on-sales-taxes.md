I&#8217;ve been seeing a lot with regards to the FairTax and Herman Cain&#8217;s 9-9-9 tax plan lately. I&#8217;m not really into posting politics here, but I&#8217;m plopping this post down on this blog and not, say, United Liberty, because this is just idle speculation on my part.

Cain&#8217;s 9-9-9 plan, [basically, is this](http://www.nationalreview.com/articles/280306/bold-brash-and-wrong-editors):

> Cain’s ultimate objective is a 30 percent national sales tax, but his interim plan is to replace the current income, payroll, and [<span style="color: #216221;">corporate tax</span>](http://www.nationalreview.com/articles/280306/bold-brash-and-wrong-editors#){#KonaLink0} codes with three new taxes. A 9 percent income tax would apply to a very different tax base: Capital income would go untaxed, but the [<span style="color: #216221;">exemptions</span>](http://www.nationalreview.com/articles/280306/bold-brash-and-wrong-editors#){#KonaLink1} that keep the basic cost of living from being taxed would disappear. A 9 percent value-added tax would be levied on corporations. And consumers would pay a 9 percent [<span style="color: #216221;">retail sales tax</span>](http://www.nationalreview.com/articles/280306/bold-brash-and-wrong-editors#){#KonaLink2} as well.

Now, there are many concerns with Cain&#8217;s 9-9-9 plan specifically, but I want to focus on something else I read, [about how a sales tax would dramatically increase the cost of food](http://www.frumforum.com/more-problems-with-cains-999-plan).

My question is: why should food be taxed under a sales tax?

One of the earliest things I remember from my economics&#8217; classes is the division between &#8220;necessities&#8221; and &#8220;luxuries.&#8221; Necessities were always defined as a trifecta of three things we all needed: food, clothing, and housing. Those were the bare essentials we needed to survive. Sure, life would probably be unpleasant, and definitely not a walk in the park, if that was all we could buy and focus on, but hey, we would be _alive_. That was the point.

To these three, I would also add healthcare and education. We need medicine to defeat viruses and get back on our feet, and well, you know, keep us _alive_. Education is a more tricky affair, since technically, one doesn&#8217;t need education to survive, but I think in the modern world it is just as essential. We rely on education in order to pull ourselves up and get forward in the world. Sadly, one can&#8217;t really get anywhere without training. (I should point out that I&#8217;m not supporting just shipping people off to college&#8211;right now, our education system is really messed up, for multiple reasons&#8211;but some sort of education is absolutely essential in order to provide for oneself and get along in life.) Having to pay taxes on that would be insane; heck, under our current system, I think you should tax _credits_ for every dollar you spend on either private schools or college.

From what I understand, the FairTax proposal&#8211;which is different from what Cain is suggesting, but similar, at least for his plan&#8217;s final stage&#8211;would include a &#8220;prebate&#8221; to offset the cost of the tax, and thus allow the poor to essentially pay no tax. But why shouldn&#8217;t we just not tax the essentials in the first place? Why worry with a prebate anyways?

I&#8217;m not sure where tax studies are on this. I haven&#8217;t found much. [Apparently, most places already don&#8217;t tax food](http://www.cbpp.org/cms/?fa=view&id=1230), so I&#8217;m not sure why it couldn&#8217;t be carried over to national policy. I&#8217;m basically putting this out here as a &#8220;food for thought&#8221; sort of thing; I&#8217;m not a tax policy expert and I will do just about everything in my power to not become one.

Of course, this is before we get to the problem that the 9-9-9 plan would basically put a national sales tax on top of a national income tax, and that would devastate people&#8217;s buying power. We&#8217;d have to repeal the 16<sup>th</sup> Amendment first. And then there&#8217;s the discussion whether or not a sales tax would be progressive or regressive. I think if we were to enact the exemptions I talked about above, then it would definitely be a progressive tax; otherwise, it might be regressive.

Anyways, just something I was thinking about.
