Like many people around the globe, Monday night I was stunned to hear that Robin Williams had died at 63. Of all the celebrity deaths that have happened in the past five years &#8211; and it sure seems like we&#8217;re killing them off at a good pace &#8211; Williams actually hit me hard. I mean, _jeez_. This is _Robin freaking Williams_ we&#8217;re talking about. He&#8217;s been around as a funny man for _ages_. I admired the man, loved his work, thought he was one of the greatest actors of my age.

And now, he&#8217;s gone. Forever.

More than the cold hard fact that he is dead, though, was _how_ he died. The sheriff&#8217;s initial report said it was suicide by asphyxiation. Williams didn&#8217;t die from disease, or an accident, or natural causes, or &#8211; heavens forbid &#8211; foul play. No, he died by his own hand, apparently to relieve whatever pain or discomfort he was going through in his life.

Williams is not the first person in my life who has chosen suicide as a way to alleviate pain.

When I was a kid, I had one friend who was my greatest friend of all. Out of respect to his family, I will not go into many details; for this story, let us call him Richard. Richard was kind of an odd duck, but he was funny, lively, and had a great imagination. He was also one of the closest friends in my life, and in many ways shaped who I am today. But in addition to this, Richard had&#8230;issues. For some time, doctors were putting him on mental medication, for reasons I could not discern. He always complained about the doctors, had arguments with them, and to be honest to my eyes I didn&#8217;t think there was anything wrong. The only issues I could ever see was that he would have low energy, or sometimes walk around and mutter strange things. But to be honest, that could describe anybody over the age of forty, so I didn&#8217;t really pay it any mind.

After awhile, he started to get better. During the latter part of my adolescence, we were two guys who weren&#8217;t part of the social mainstream in any way, but we functioned and we continued. We had fun, we talked about serious topics, even played D&D (once) and Halo (a lot). I really thought Richard was on the up and up, and soon he would be going off to college and becoming a nuclear engineer in the military.

I was 18 when I heard the news. I was driving home from my summer job as a contract archivist. I pulled in the driveway, up to the garage door, when my mother comes running down the stairs, the phone in her hand, crying hysterically. She told me that Richard had shot himself, that he was dead, and I should probably go see his family.

He lived down the road from me, so I walked. I was just stunned. To this day, one of the things that really stuck out in my memory was that I didn&#8217;t shed a single tear. Instead, I just walked down the road with my mouth wide open, my body shaking in a silent scream. I was just so _empty_. I thought something was wrong with me. But no, I realized afterwards, there was nothing wrong with me. This was just how I was dealing with it.

I didn&#8217;t see his body until the service; I just saw it taken out on a gurney, wrapped inside of a bag. He left behind a note, a rather cryptic one, and all that was left was to pick up the pieces. I remember talking with somebody &#8211; I can&#8217;t remember who &#8211; that I was really surprised. &#8220;He was doing better,&#8221; I said. &#8220;Why would he do this?&#8221;

&#8220;Sometimes,&#8221; this other person said, &#8220;It&#8217;s when they&#8217;re coming up from the depths that they decide to do this. Because when they&#8217;re _really_ down, they can&#8217;t get up the energy to pull the trigger. It&#8217;s only when they get enough to do it, that&#8217;s when it happens.&#8221;

I&#8217;m paraphrasing, but the basic point stands. You may think they&#8217;re doing better. You may think they&#8217;re &#8220;all right.&#8221; But nobody knows what truly goes on in the hearts and minds of other people. What you get, at best, is an edited simulacrum. So take the time to talk to people. Take an hour of your time and really understand them. _Listen_ to them. Be there for them. If you truly care, then they are _more_ than worth an hour of your life. My friend was certainly worth an hour. Hell, he was worth an entire _month_. I would have gladly taken that time to talk to him about his issues.

These people are trying to find peace from all their pain and suffering. That&#8217;s all. It&#8217;s not selfish, cowardly, or anything at all like that. In the state they are in, they are suffering deeply, and they need peace &#8211; and when they get to this point, suicide is the only option that occurs to them.

So do that. If someone you know is depressed, or worse perhaps suicidal &#8211; or _even if they don&#8217;t appear to be at all_ &#8211; take a moment. Talk with them. Maybe take an entire hour, or more. Just talk with them, let them know you&#8217;re there and you want to help. That&#8217;s all. I didn&#8217;t do that because I didn&#8217;t recognize what was happening. Don&#8217;t share the same regret I feel today.
