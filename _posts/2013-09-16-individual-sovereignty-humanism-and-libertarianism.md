I apologize for this post; it&#8217;s a bit spread out because of the way I got to the topic in question.

Over the weekend, NYPD officers attempted to subdue an apparently crazy person in Times Square. I say attempted, because all they really did [was bulletspray and hit two completely innocent bystanders](http://www.cbsnews.com/8301-201_162-57602998/times-square-shooting-nypd-officers-shoot-two-innocent-bystanders-near-times-square/)&#8211;one an elderly lady in a walker. This, after the bulletspray fest [last year outside the Empire State Building](http://www.huffingtonpost.com/2012/08/25/empire-state-building-shooting-nypd-bullets-shot-all-nine_n_1830007.html), and also earlier this year when the LAPD, during the Dorner manhunt, [bulletsprayed a completely random car that didn&#8217;t look anything like the vehicle that was on their APB](http://www.theatlanticwire.com/national/2013/04/women-mistaken-chris-dorner-and-shot-lapd-will-get-42-million/64513/).

Naturally, I spoke out against this. I think police brutality and police incompetence are serious issues that deserve more of a national discussion. Unlike talk about the deficit and the debt ceiling, or foreign policy, or the minutiae of economic regulation, police reform and criminal justice reform touches on Americans directly. It affects citizens in a very immediate sense&#8211;[usually by killing them](http://www.cnn.com/2013/09/15/justice/north-carolina-police-shooting/index.html). Yet for some reason, despite all the deaths logged by the [Cato Institute&#8217;s National Police Misconduct Reporting Project](http://www.policemisconduct.net/), or the other horrendous activities reported by [CopBlock](http://www.copblock.org), or the [crazy stories of civil asset forfeiture run amok](http://www.newyorker.com/reporting/2013/08/12/130812fa_fact_stillman), or even [Third Amendment violations](http://www.outsidethebeltway.com/police-take-over-private-home-giving-rise-to-possible-third-amendment-violation/), it seems to me that nobody is really talking about this in a meaningful way. Politicians sweep it under the rug and go on to start another shouting match about the debt ceiling or them brown people coming over the border.

I also noted that one of the scariest things that is happening is the militarization of police around the country, something I noted last year for United Liberty, and which is the subject of a recent book by Radley Balko. This led to a gun control activist to start yelling about how the police were arming themselves with military hardware because the NRA had weakened gun laws and led to [rampant armament of the populace](https://twitter.com/MamaRedfield/status/379089937384996864). Naturally, I disagreed. We had a bit of a back and forth about it, and then somehow suicide entered into the equation. I&#8217;ll let my Storify speak for itself:

This leads me to the real meat and potatoes of this blog post, which is atheism, libertarianism, and what it really means to be a humanist.

A long time ago, [I blogged about the silliness that is Atheism+](http://jdkolassa.net/2012/09/the-absurdity-of-atheismplus/), a new &#8220;movement&#8221; that tried to merge atheism with third-wave feminism and far-left progressivism by basically being assholes to everybody. One of the most important points is that atheism does not lead to anything directly. Atheism, being merely the rejection of belief in a supernatural entity or entities, doesn&#8217;t really entail anything beyond that. Even though I would really like to agree [with this blog post that says atheism leads to libertarianism](http://blog.theproudatheist.com/should-atheism-and-libertarianism-go-hand-in-hand-part-2/), even that is really not true. How, exactly, does lack of a belief in a supernatural entity lead to a libertarian leap? It doesn&#8217;t. There is no underlying philosophical foundation there. The previous author talks about controlling your own life and thinking for yourself, but that is not _ipso facto_ atheism.

There is a difference, though, between atheism and humanism. Atheism is a philosophical position. Humanism is to atheism what Christianity is to theism (sort of). While there is a long running argument over whether or not humanism is a religion (other terms include &#8220;life stance,&#8221; a &#8220;replacement for religion,&#8221; which I think both works and yet doesn&#8217;t), it sort of fits the bill. Just barely.

What is humanism, though? Let&#8217; see a couple of definitions:

<div>
  <ol>
    <li>
      (Philosophy) the denial of any power or moral value superior to that of humanity; the rejection of religion in favour of a belief in the advancement of humanity by its own efforts
    </li>
    <li>
      (Philosophy) a philosophical position that stresses the autonomy of human reason in contradistinction to the authority of the Church
    </li>
  </ol>
  
  <p>
    Note that: human <em>reason</em>. Human <em>autonomy</em>. Exactly the things that I mentioned above in my Twitter rant.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    Yes, said autonomy sometimes includes suicide. This is a tragic thing, but yet if we&#8217;re going to respect autonomy then we must respect that too. But for the most part, that doesn&#8217;t happen, that doesn&#8217;t come up. What does come up all the time are small things, small decisions. Like the size of soda cup you&#8217;re buying, or your sexual orientation, or what sort of clothes you like to wear.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    These decisions stem from our sapience, and come from our rationality. And if you&#8217;re going to be a human being, and not reject humanity, then you <em>must</em> embrace this sapience, and moreover, individual human sovereignty. Anything else is inhuman, full stop.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    That&#8217;s why I think libertarianism and humanism naturally go together. If you&#8217;re a libertarian, that leads to humanism because you&#8217;re focused on freeing individuals from the power of a large government, and letting them control their lives; and humanism is all about human lives being front and center. If you&#8217;re a humanist, focusing on human lives and <em>humanity</em>, then you should naturally be a libertarian, because libertarianism embraces and encourages the natural essence of humanity, sapience.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    I&#8217;ve been thinking about this topic for a long time now ever since I heard about &#8220;thick libertarianism.&#8221; This is the idea that libertarianism entails other ideas that are not necessarily political, that there are consequences to being a libertarian. The idea, as far as I can determine, was formed by Charles Johnson, also known as RadGeek, a left-libertarian blogger. Here is a good reading list to start on if you want to know more about thick libertarianism and libertarian morality:
  </p>
  
  <ol>
    <li>
      The post where it all (sorta) began, &#8220;<a href="http://radgeek.com/gt/2008/10/03/libertarianism_through/">Libertarianism Through Thick & Thin</a>&#8221; by Charles Johnson
    </li>
    <li>
      &#8220;<a href="http://bleedingheartlibertarians.com/2011/12/libertarianism-thick-and-thin/">Libertarianism: Thick and Thin</a>&#8220;, by Matt Zwolinski
    </li>
    <li>
      &#8220;<a href="http://bleedingheartlibertarians.com/2011/12/libertarianism-and-morality/">Libertarianism and Moralit</a>y&#8221; by Fernando Teson
    </li>
    <li>
      &#8220;<a href="http://bleedingheartlibertarians.com/2013/02/libertarian-social-morality-progressive-conservative-or-liberal/">Libertarian Social Morality: Progressive, Conservative, or Liberal?</a>&#8221; by Kevin Vallier
    </li>
    <li>
      BONUS: &#8220;<a href="http://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2221998">The Libertarian Middle Way</a>&#8220;, by Randy Barnett
    </li>
  </ol>
  
  <p>
    Johnson explores several different forms of thick libertarianism, or shades of thickness, really. Two of these are &#8220;strategic thickness&#8211;causes of liberty,&#8221; and &#8220;thickness from consequences&#8211;the effects of liberty.&#8221; I think both of these lead toward humanism. The first because, as Johnson himself notes:
  </p>
  
  <blockquote>
    <p>
      Or, to take a less controversial example, many if not most libertarians, throughout the history of the movement, have argued that there are good reasons for libertarians to promote a culture in which reason and independent thinking are highly valued, and blind conformism is treated with contempt. But if this is a good thing for liberty, it must be for reasons other than some kind of entailment of the non-aggression principle. Certainly everyone has a <em>right</em> to believe things simply because <q>everybody</q> believes it, or to do things simply because <q>everybody</q> does it, as long as their conformism respects the equal rights of independent thinkers to think independently and act independently with their own person and property. It is logically conceivable that a society could be <em>rigidly</em> conformist while remaining entirely free; it would just have to be the case that the individual people within that society were, by and large, psychologically and culturally inclined to be so docile, and so sensitive to social disapproval, ostracism, and verbal peer pressure, that they all voluntarily chose to go along with the crowd.
    </p>
  </blockquote>
  
  <p>
    Technically, reason itself doesn&#8217;t require libertarianism, but if we&#8217;re going to promote a society where there is limited government and people have individual responsibility for their own actions, then you&#8217;re going to promote reason. And when you do that, you find yourself heading towards freethought, which heads towards humanism&#8230;
  </p>
  
  <p>
    The other, &#8220;effects of liberty,&#8221; is simply the same thing but in reverse. A society of free people is going to lead towards humanism in one way or another. If we&#8217;re going to give people power over their own lives, there is going to be less power from the Church.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    I&#8217;m not saying that one cannot be a Christian and a libertarian at the same time, but there is a tension there between the Christian and libertarian elements that I don&#8217;t think you get from being an atheist libertarian or a humanist one. For centuries, the Christian Church has been a state unto itself, passing edicts and laws and being very forceful in demanding people to bow to its will, or at least the will of whomever at the time was wearing the most outrageous hat. God is described as a king, with ultimate power, and everyone is to bow down and obey him. Indeed, for a long time, free will was ignored, and the Church was extremely authoritative. Although various Christian denominations have undergone rebranding efforts over the past couple of centuries, dealing with the rise of (classical) liberalism, Christianity is still very much a top-down, hierarchical, authoritative institution. &#8220;Follow our commands or burn in hell forever.&#8221; Not exactly a lot of leeway there.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    I should also point out that I don&#8217;t exactly agree with many of the various &#8220;Humanist Manifestos&#8221; either. A lot of what I&#8217;ve seen published suggests that many want to make humanism lean towards some variety of socialism or social democracy&#8211;but then, I see these people as not being fully humanist either. If they&#8217;re going to take so many decisions away from individuals and put it in the hands of a nebulous, all-powerful state, then they&#8217;re not embracing the very essence of humanity either. Just because I use the term &#8220;humanist&#8221; doesn&#8217;t mean I&#8217;m talking about the party line of the American Humanist Association, the Council for Secular Humanism, or the IHEU. I&#8217;m talking solely about a human-centered philosophy that lacks supernatural elements.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    That, by itself, I think goes hand in hand with libertarianism. Sort of an odd topic to come to via police brutality and suicide, but that&#8217;s what happens when something has been bubbling under the surface for awhile and gets hit with a random act of tragedy.
  </p>
</div>
