In this day of smartphones, iPads, cloud software, and 8-core CPUs, I find that the best tool to write a novel or a story is a notebook. No no, you fool, not an Alienware m14 (though that is very attractive), I mean a _notebook_. You know, a book, where you write notes in it.

Don&#8217;t get me wrong, I am a very, very big fan of computers and technology. I actually, most likely, have an unhealthy addiction to computers. I&#8217;m already looking at upgrading away from my current laptop (an Averatec 2500), which weighs about 17,256 pounds, and getting something like a netbook or perhaps, an ultrabook, something around 10&#8243;-11.6&#8243; so it will fit in a small backpack, so I can carry it around DC. And my current laptop is only about four years old.*

But the truth of the matter is, computers are really quite terrible to write novels on. They&#8217;re excellent when it comes to storing copies and drafts, and actually putting in the &#8220;final&#8221; product. Cloud storage is even better, because then you can save it on the web, behind passwords and security, and don&#8217;t have to worry if your laptop or notebook goes missing; your work has not been lost (except for the most recent stuff that hasn&#8217;t been backed up.) However, I find that when you&#8217;re actually muddling through a story, trying to figure out where to go next, a computer is a bloody awful device.

First you have to log on and sign in. Wait, let me back up. First, you have to wait for your computer to boot up. If you have one of those &#8220;instant-on&#8221; Linux operating systems installed on top of your Windows or Mac OSX install, or a solid state drive, this isn&#8217;t really that long. But god forbid if you have a mechanical hard disk drive and just Windows as your boot, like most computer users&#8211;you&#8217;ll be sitting there for what feels like an eternity. Sure, it&#8217;s not really that long&#8211;maybe 40-90 seconds&#8211;but wasting even that much amount of time spinning your wheels is dreadful.

Okay, so your computer is booted up. Then you have to log on and sign in. Then you have to navigate to your writing software. (Who knows how long that&#8217;ll take; if you&#8217;re smart, its pinned to your taskbar or your start list. If you&#8217;re not&#8230;) Then you&#8217;ll have to find your file. And&#8211;oh, look, an email from your girlfriend. Oh, and somebody mentioned you on Twitter. And jeebus, there&#8217;s another system update to use. On and on and on&#8230;

So while I&#8217;m fairly young and ensconced in the &#8220;tech generation,&#8221; I still like to have my old standby, the [Mead](http://www.meadonline.com/products/notebooks.aspx). You can get it in 7&#8243;, 8&#8243;, 9.5&#8243; or 11&#8243; configurations, with anywhere between 60, 70, or 180 pages of storage. (Sure, if you think of each page as a gigabyte, that&#8217;s not a lot, but it&#8217;s a whole heck of a lot cheaper, even as cheap as digital storage is.) And I find that&#8217;s really all I need.

As the lead hits the paper, ideas quite literally flow. And if you decide you don&#8217;t like something, you can erase right there. Ideas just don&#8217;t flow from my mind so easily on a computer screen, which is probably a result of the eyestrain and the rapid hand movements I have to constantly make. (Also, I have big, fat fingers, and no keyboard is ever large enough for them.) I find myself, especially when blogging, getting up and running around my room to process thoughts, rather than just sitting there and _writing_ them _out_.

This, of course, before we get to distractions.

Ultimately, this is just me. I know that. Other writers may never have a need to turn to paper and pencil, but I just feel&#8211;in this era of rapidly advancing technology, which I am very supportive of and pleased with&#8211;there is a real need to go back to basis and back to the way things were before. Not every advancement is necessarily good. Not every new thing can properly be termed &#8220;progress.&#8221;

Now, where did I put that external drive&#8230;
