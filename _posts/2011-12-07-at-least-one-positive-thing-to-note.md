One of those things that you always get in writing books or writing lessons is about developing your &#8220;voice.&#8221; It&#8217;s not something easy to define or explain; it&#8217;s always by example. For instance, the feel and tone of J.R.R. Tolkien&#8217;s works is vastly different from James Patterson. It&#8217;s a combination of word choice, rhythm, inflection, slang.

For years, I have struggled with voice, because it seems that every time I write, I end up soaking up my favorite writers and writing too much like them. It&#8217;s not _my_ voice, I&#8217;m just ripping off someone else, and it doesn&#8217;t feel genuine.

But last night, while working on a novel (not as far as I wanted to get, but far enough), I realized that it was different. I just started reading _A Madness of Angels, Or, The Resurrection of Matthew Swift_ by Kate Griffin (apparently a pseudonym for Catherine Webb), and the difference was huge&#8211;and fortunately, not one I will be copying. Don&#8217;t get me wrong, Griffin is a good writer, but she goes a bit too far into description and purple prose for my taste, until I feel like I&#8217;m swimming in syrup.

I&#8217;ve also noticed, though, that it doesn&#8217;t copy Jim Butcher, Aaron Allston, or Isaac Adamson, my three favorite authors, either. It&#8217;s seems, relatively speaking, a bit too plain, yet at the same time has a sense of vitality and intrigue to it. And plain is not bad; what it means is that it just isn&#8217;t pretentious. (Not that I would say anybody&#8217;s writing is really pretentious, other than academic texts, and a few literary authors whom I won&#8217;t name.)

I have a draft post I&#8217;ve set aside for awhile on some really good books on writing, some of which have entire chapters on voice. I&#8217;ll see about getting that up and showing you what I mean.

(And I&#8217;ll think about posting excerpts and letting you decide what you think of them. But only if my nonexistent readership bribes me.)
