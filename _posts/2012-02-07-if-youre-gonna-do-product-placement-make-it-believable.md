There&#8217;s nothing I hate more than arrogance and people trying to make me feel stupid. Well, actually, I could probably list a dozen more things I hate (procrastination, Republicans, Democrats, crony capitalism, socialism, the lack of giant robots, the high price of&#8230;everything&#8230;etc.) but those seem a bit more personal to me than other things. Aside from the giant robots. That one is _all_ me.

Increasingly common in TV and film productions these days are product placement, when you see objects that very clearly are a company&#8217;s mark in a show. _Eureka_ started doing this in&#8211;I think&#8211;season three when they had a bunch of extras working at the top-secret research facility wear Degree Mens&#8217; logos on their jumpsuits. That was explained away by the new manager, who argued that they had to have product placement in order to help pay the bills (since their federal funding was cut.) That&#8217;s great; although it may seem questionable to have corporate branding and corporate advertising inside a top-secret Area 51 type place, that part was explained away by _Eureka_&#8216;s tone as a comedy that didn&#8217;t take itself too seriously all the time. Secondly, the in-universe explanation tied it into the show, hung a lamp on it (to use some movemaking slang), and really made it feel like it fit and belonged there. It made the show&#8217;s universe feel more real.

Then let&#8217;s get to my favorite, _Fringe_. One bit of product placement that turned up a lot last year were Sprint&#8217;s new smartphones and their video chat technology (ha, take _that_, Apple FaceTime! Yeah! Or something like that.) That also wasn&#8217;t a problem, because it was quite subtle&#8211;just a smartphone with the Sprint logo, and then sometimes they used the video chat. The worst part about it was that the video quality was a bazillion times better than what you will ever get in a billion years (because it was all pre-filmed and then added into the scene in post-production.) Nothing wrong there. Very believable and made the world seem more accurate and deep.

I particularly like this literary technique; I&#8217;ve seen it described as &#8220;K-mart realism,&#8221; where authors will use everyday, mundane objects, products, branding, etc., to make the world they are creating more round and rich and deep and just something that you can believe actually exist. Now, if you work in film or TV, there a multitude of other concerns&#8211;namely, corporations can get upset if you use a product in a way that looks badly on the product&#8211;but sometimes it works out.

Sometimes, however, it&#8217;s just dumb.

In the current season, the Fringe protagonists have been using a Nissan Leaf to get around. Now, I don&#8217;t know any current or former FBI agents, but I would be very, _very_ surprised to learn that an FBI agent had a Nissan Leaf as a personal car, let alone using it while on duty to investigate crimes.

(For those who don&#8217;t know what a [Nissan Leaf](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nissan_Leaf) is, it is basically a tiny egg-shaped contraption that is powered by an electric motor. It is not a hybrid&#8211;it is a completely, 100%, electric vehicle. _Top Gear_ reviewed it and a competitor from Renault, and found it&#8230;well, wanting sounds kind of wanting, but it&#8217;s also pretty accurate. Basically, they drove it maybe fifty miles, and then had to wait approximately a day before it would charge up to be usable again. Not exactly something an FBI would want to use on the job. Or any human being, for that matter.)

Think about it. What if you had to engage in a car chase? Sure, you might get up to 80&#8230;for all of about twenty seconds, before the car&#8217;s battery died. And what is up with the journeys between Boston and the Hudson Valley (in New York)? Viewers sort of tolerated the magical two hour journeys between the Big Apple and the Big Dig because it was part of the tradition of dramatic time&#8211;that is, time moves as fast or as slow as the narrative demands&#8211;but come _on_, there is no way a Nissan Leaf is going to make it to Boston to Connecticut to some area just south of Albany New York (where the show&#8217;s fictional &#8220;Reiden Lake&#8221; is located) and back again on the casual charging they seem to do. You might be able to travel between universes, create bubbles in time, develop advanced cloning and precognition, and maybe even make chicken parmesean ice cream, but an electric car going that sort of distance? Give. Me. A. _<span style="text-decoration: underline;"><strong>Break</strong></span>_.

I otherwise have no problems with either _Fringe_ or the Leaf; I will continue to watch and enjoy the show (though this &#8220;Alt-SuperTimeline&#8221; thing is getting a bit exhausting) and if I end up staying in the DC metro area and not go on any long-distance excursions, I might end up buying a Leaf to avoid emission taxation (which I am _sure_ is coming down the pipeline) and high gas prices, but together, in that way? Oh come on. It just doesn&#8217;t mix.

I understand there may be other considerations&#8211;maybe they _really really_ need the money&#8211;but please, if you&#8217;re going place a product, make it at least somewhat believable. We&#8217;re not _that_ dumb.

Except the people watching _Jersey Shore_.
