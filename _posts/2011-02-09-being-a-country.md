Life&#8217;s Little Mysteries has a post up on [how the new Republic of South Sudan will become a country](http://www.lifeslittlemysteries.com/how-are-new-countries-established-1323/). It is apparently 3 &#8220;simple&#8221; steps: declare independence, gain recognition, and join the UN.

I disagree.

All that is really required to be a state is to have a monopoly on the use of force. [As Murray Rothbard explains](http://mises.org/easaran/chap3.asp),

> One method of the birth of a State may be illustrated as follows: in the hills of southern &#8220;Ruritania,&#8221; a bandit group manages to obtain physical control over the territory, and finally the bandit chieftain proclaims himself &#8220;King of the sovereign and independent government of South Ruritania&#8221;; and, if he and his men have the force to maintain this rule for a while, lo and behold! a new State has joined the &#8220;family of nations,&#8221; and the former bandit leaders have been transformed into the lawful nobility of the realm.

All that it really takes for a country to become a country is if it can hold on to its territory or not. If it can maintain territorial integrity, fight off aggressors, and police its populace (thus suppressing any domestic challenges to its rule) it is a state, a country. What does the diplomatic recognition of another country really mean? Did anyone formally &#8220;recognize&#8221; the Roman Empire? The United States does not recognize Taiwan as a country&#8211;preferring to instead give diplomatic recognition to the Chinese mainland, something I feel is a mistake&#8211;but do any Americans seriously think Taiwan is not a country and, say, a province of the People&#8217;s Republic of China? I think not.

With regards to the modern diplomatic system, yes, those three steps do count, but they actually have nothing to do with being a _bona fide_ country or not. They just grant you membership into a certain club, one that is now so wide that everyone takes it for granted, but it doesn&#8217;t actually say whether you&#8217;re a state or not.

It all comes down to force (and money, which usually makes the force possible), that &#8220;monopoly on violence.&#8221; This doesn&#8217;t mean that I believe in or prefer the absolute monopoly of force&#8211;I believe there is a lot of room for personal defense and private security, more of the &#8220;relative&#8221; monopoly of force idea; as long as the state can keep out any violence it deems against its own order, it&#8217;s good to go. Now, some may bring up the violence rates in the United States and say &#8220;Oh, well by your argument the US isn&#8217;t a country either,&#8221; but let&#8217;s be honest here, violent crime has been steadily dropping for the past 20 years, and none of that violence is seriously challenging Washington&#8217;s order. If we had a non-negligible national revolution going on&#8211;a civil war&#8211;then there would be some question. During the Civil War, for instance, the CSA was an actual country, while the Union was only a country in the north, where it had actual control over its territory.

Why people continual to think in these contrived ways, rather than simply accept cold, hard reality, is something I will never understand. Unless they pick up one of my stories.
