> &#8220;In my heart, I’m an [anarchist](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_anarchism), meaning that I don’t think states have the moral authority to rule and I don’t think I have any duty to obey. Anarchy would be the _morally_ best system, **but that doesn’t mean we should overthrow all the existing states, or even try to approximate anarchy**. **That would probably be a disaster.** So, given that we have states that coerce and force and push people around, they should refrain from enforcing a system of property that makes some people so badly off that they cannot lead a minimally decent life.&#8221;

The above quote is from Jessica Flanigan of Bleeding Heart Libertarians. The bold part is mine; the italics are hers. It&#8217;s very similar to where I stand on anarchism; I am, in general, a minarchist, though I don&#8217;t think the state _ipso facto_ has a greater moral hold on you than anybody else. I just happen to think the same way as Robert Nozick&#8211;that a state is very likely inevitable, that it would be desirable to have a minimal state to deal with disputes, and that as long as it doesn&#8217;t start violating what he calls &#8220;side constraints&#8221; (or what we call &#8220;freedoms,&#8221; basically) than it is justified. Essentially, I&#8217;m a realist when it comes to politics.

Taken from a post on [universal basic incomes](http://bleedingheartlibertarians.com/2012/04/bhls-ubis/); while I&#8217;ve been cleaning out my apartment I came across my political books, namely John Tomasi&#8217;s _[Free Market Fairness](http://www.amazon.com/Free-Market-Fairness-John-Tomasi/dp/069114446X)_, remembered he said that Hayek was in support of a [universal basic income](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basic_income_guarantee), and looked for the money quote.

Part of me really likes a UBI; if we just had that instead of all the other forms of welfare, and a fairly light regulatory scheme, a la the &#8220;[social insurance state](http://bleedingheartlibertarians.com/2012/05/the-administrative-state-vs-the-social-insurance-state/),&#8221; I would be pretty groovy (so long as the government otherwise restricted itself to public goods such as defense, police, prisons, judiciary, and roads.) However, I recognize that would never be feasible; even in the US a universal basic income that would make sense (say, $1000 a month) would utterly dwarf our economy. It would be larger than our spending today. So I&#8217;m afraid I have to pass, although I might be open to the following:

  1. Means-tested guaranteed minimum income 
      1. Benefits restricted to those 150% of poverty line and below
      2. Recipient must be on &#8220;reintegration trajectory&#8221; (i.e., actually improving themselves) to receive benefits
  2. Privatized &#8220;social security&#8221; acccounts

That would be a lot better than what we have now. And no, I don&#8217;t know how to get there from here. Or even how to properly run it.
