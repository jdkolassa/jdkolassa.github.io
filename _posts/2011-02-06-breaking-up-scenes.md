You know, sometimes I find it difficult to break up scenes. Or, more accurately, to break up plot lines. For example, right now I&#8217;m working on a story where the first half&#8211;or maybe first two thirds&#8211;depends on how it comes out&#8211;focuses on two different characters in two different places, who eventually come together. I&#8217;m trying to juggle these two characters and their focus, not wanting one to take over completely.

The problem is, I&#8217;m having exactly that occur. I have character A and his &#8220;plot line&#8221; get involved in a fight, break that up to focus on character B doing some light investigating&#8211;this is another trick you should know if you&#8217;re going to be a writer, the &#8220;soap opera tactic&#8221;&#8211;and then go back to A, but unfortunately, having B come back after a little while would be just a bit&#8230;well, let&#8217;s just say &#8220;problematic&#8221; and leave it at that. Where does one break up the action? And how long does one focus on it before switching?

To me, common sense would dictate that you focus on each character for as short as is possible. Get the reader hooked in, interested in what&#8217;s happening&#8211;then switch to the other character, and leave the reader hanging. Now, it&#8217;s possible that the reader will be pissed. Do it too much and the reader is going to be one annoyed nerf herder. Don&#8217;t do it at all and the reader will be satisfied when you complete that storyline, will feel that&#8217;s enough for now, and put the iPad down and go to sleep.

For those of you not in the know, _this is a very bad thing_. You don&#8217;t want your reader to stop reading, you want him or her to keep going and going and never put it down. You have to strike a balance, whereby the reader can&#8217;t stop reading because he or she wants to figure out what&#8217;s going on with the character you switched from, and so tries to read through this other stuff to get to back to his or her interest, but then he or she* gets interested in character B, and now you switch back to A, and thus she has to keep reading because she&#8217;s interested in both&#8230;.this is what they mean when they say &#8220;It was so good I couldn&#8217;t put it down!&#8221; This is that soap opera tactic I was talking about earlier. And believe me, having read through riveting stories of X-Wing fighter pilots by Michael J. Stackpole and Aaron Allston, not to mention some other ludicrously amazing science fiction stories by Kevin J. Anderson and brilliantly weaved tales of urban mystery by Jim Butcher, I can tell you with all honesty that it just _works_. And when you&#8217;re trying to get published, that&#8217;s what you need to do.

Not that it&#8217;s easy, though. Not by a long shot. And there&#8217;s really no formula, no algebraic equation that says &#8220;put so much time here, so much there, question yourself for a bit, then, profit!&#8221; It takes a bit of guesswork. For instance, I was reading _Star Wars: X-Wing: Iron Fist_ a little while ago (yes, that series was afflicted with colon cancer; we commemorate it&#8217;s death every year as a message for all new series to take care of their titles) and we&#8217;re confronted with a commando group being led by Wedge Antilles trying to break into an Imperial base. Just after they get in, the narrative switches to another pilot, far away, being reprimanded for being a fool during a simulator run. Now, this character was contacted by the main cast a while back, and only _now_ are they getting to her. Her spotlight is rather brief, before they&#8217;re back with the main cast, and it stays that way for a while. However, since this is a novel, with a far larger cast of characters, then can jump around a bit more than I can, since I&#8217;m only focusing on two.

This is probably going to trip me up a lot on this story, keep me from submitting it as early as I would prefer to those magazines. But, I guess, that would just mean it&#8217;s better, right?

_*I think from now on, I&#8217;m just going to label all of my readers as &#8220;she.&#8221; Makes things easier that way. And more appealing._
