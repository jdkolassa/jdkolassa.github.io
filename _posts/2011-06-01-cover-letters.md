Grr, grr, grr.

Writing a story with believable characters, a strong plot, and delightful dialogue is hard. Writing a cover letter to get a job is just murder.

I&#8217;ve been writing several of them over the past week, aiming for a day job here in the nation&#8217;s capital. (A necessary evil, I&#8217;m afraid.) I&#8217;ve been trying to take my accumulated experience and education and distill it into a one page account that sells me to a prospective employer, but as that clause points out so well, I&#8217;ve been writing too much. A cover letter should be like flash fiction, I&#8217;m told: short and to the point. Oh, and don&#8217;t copy your resume.

Unfortunately, A) I&#8217;ve sort of been doing that and B) there is nowhere near a consensus on what a cover letter should really look like. Some say you need bullets in your letter, others say you should tell a story, still more say you should avoid that, and just about everyone has their own way of formatting it. That&#8217;s okay, I suppose, because writing is a personal thing, and is endlessly &#8220;customizable,&#8221; but for chrissakes, this is a job application, not a novel.

I think we need to come to some sort of consensus on what a cover letter is supposed to be and stick to that. This is utterly confusing to someone new to the job market, and I don&#8217;t think it helps anybody, either the new worker or the employer looking to fill positions (nor the statistics hacks at the Bureau of Labor Statistics who want U-3 to keep going down.) At least, it sure as heck doesn&#8217;t help me.

(Oh, and another pet peeve: companies that force you to copy and paste your resume and cover letter into a text box, so naturally, it looks like crap. Why can&#8217;t companies take PDFs nowadays? Hello, this is the second decade of the 21st century. Let&#8217;s get with the program. No pun intended.)
